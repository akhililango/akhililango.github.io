Working papers

Consumer Search and Paid Placement  (Paper, Slides)  
I develop a general theory for digital marketplaces where an intermediary provides the platform for firms to list their product, and for consumers to search for products in this list. The intermediary determines the order in which products are displayed, by conducting a Generalised Second-Price (GSP) auction. The effect of the auction on product prices is captured by the reserve price, which is chosen by the intermediary to maximise its ad commission from firms. In equilibrium, the firm at the top of the list charges a higher price, earns a larger profit and pays higher ad commission. In this setting, I show that consumer surplus increases with search cost as it restrains consumers from searching inefficiently. However, the dispersion between firms' profit also increases with search cost. In one specification, I find that setting an upper bound on auction reserve price improves consumer surplus by 63%, without affecting the overall welfare. These findings suggest that a significant share of the ad commission that firms pay the intermediary are passed on to consumers.

The Demand Side of Resource (Mis)allocation in India with Sampreet Goraya  (Paper, Slides)  
Local demand is an important determinant of firm growth in rural areas. In this project, we document the role of ethnic networks in segmenting the local demand and its implication for local firm growth and aggregate misallocation. We focus our analysis on India, where the caste system plays a significant role on individuals' economic outcomes. First, we show that demand is segmented along caste lines. This, in turn, creates markup and productivity dispersion across firms owned by different castes. To identify this demand segmentation effect, we use exogenous changes in regional rainfall intensity as an instrument for local demand shocks. We find that the Low Caste (LC) consumers experience a positive income shock due to rainfall and that this translates into higher per-capita consumption for them. Further, we show that regional increase in the LCs' demand translates into higher output growth and lower markup for the firms with LC owners' relative to others. Using a quantitative model, we highlight the importance of demand channel in determining firm growth and misallocation.
