Sponsored Search
ABC, 2024

Abstract  Slides   Paper   Supplement   Code  Data

 I develop a general theory for digital marketplaces where an intermediary provides the platform for firms to list their product, and for consumers to search for products in this list. The intermediary determines the order in which products are displayed, by conducting a Generalised Second-Price (GSP) auction. The effect of the auction on product prices is captured by the reserve price, which is chosen by the intermediary to maximise its ad commission from firms. In equilibrium, the firm at the top of the list charges a higher price, earns a larger profit and pays higher ad commission. In this setting, I show that consumer surplus increases with search cost as it restrains consumers from searching inefficiently. However, the dispersion between firms' profit also increases with search cost. In one specification, I find that setting an upper bound on auction reserve price improves consumer surplus by 63%, without affecting the overall welfare. These findings suggest that a significant share of the ad commission that firms pay the intermediary are passed on to consumers.
